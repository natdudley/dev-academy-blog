Problem solving techniques

27 November 2019

Describe a time you got blocked on a simple problem. What was the problem, what problem solving techniques did you use, how did you feel throughout the process, and what did you learn?

During sprint 4 of DevAcademy, we had a series of JavaScript Kata to complete. These were small coding challenges. I blasted through most of them on the first day. I was near the end, and it was past 6 PM in the evening, when I was asked to take an input on an array (a list of numbers) and calculate the average of that array.

I knew it wasn’t a hard problem to solve so I skipped writing pseudocode and went straight to it, but the combination of tiredness and a new format with arrow notation and const (which results in a different way of laying out a function) meant I couldn’t figure it out. I went round and round in circles, console.logging everything, but getting more and more irritated. I decided to listen to my brain telling me I was tired and hungry and close my laptop for the night, even though I hate leaving a problem unsolved.

After dinner, I described the problem to my partner, talking through how I was going to approach solving it the next day. The next morning, I sat down, wrote some pseudocode that followed the approach I’d figured out, and then wrote some actual code with step-by-step logging, and immediately was able to see the right path forward.

It felt good to solve it, but also it felt good to have recognised when I was too tired to work and to have made the sensible decision to try again with a fresh brain, and not beat myself up about it. I learned that it’s important to listen to your body, and that even when it seems simple, writing pseudocode is a good idea.

Describe a time you solved a problem in an elegant way. What was the problem, what problem solving techniques did you use, how did you feel throughout the process, and what did you learn?

During the same sprint, one of the Kata asked us to “unjumble” a sentence which was written backwards. Before I started, I went and looked at the MDN docs to see what types of methods (like a technique) I could use to achieve this, and reading through these gave me an idea of how to approach the problem. I then wrote out pseudocode working through this step by step. Then, I started writing code, adding logging to every step so I could check what the outcomes were. Once I got to the correct outcome, I was delighted, but that delight was soon dashed by the tests not passing.

I was really confused, because I could see I was getting the right answer, but I wondered if maybe it was because there was a different way of solving it. I took a step back, and rethought my approach, and refactored my code to make it more simple and elegant. Like before, it output the correct answer, but still the tests didn’t pass. I was really frustrated at this point! I googled, but to no avail. I could not figure out why the test was failing.

I asked a peer for help, and together, we realised there was a problem with one of the tests. When we ran it in Chrome dev tools, it was fine, but in the tool we were using, it failed. They helped me rewrite the test so it would work in the environment.

Whilst I was frustrated at this, ultimately the tests failing made me go back and look at the problem again, and see a much nicer way to write the code.

Reflect on how confident you feel using the problem solving techniques and process:

Pseudocode

I feel confident using this, and I regularly use this technique. I just have to remember not to skip it because a problem seems easy.

Trying something

I will totally always try something. This, combined with logging, can help to reduce uncertainty in the problem.

Rubber ducky method

I feel a bit silly doing this, especially in a shared space, but when I do it it helps a lot.

Reading error messages

I’m a bit less confident with this, but I do read them to see what they say and the line they occur on.

Console.logging

Always be console logging! I use this all the time.

Googling

Google is totally your best friend, although you have to pay close attention to the quality of the sources and how recently they were written in case they use deprecated approaches.

Asking your peers for help

I’ve done a bit of this, although it’s a bit harder when you’re a little ahead in the curriculum.

Asking coaches for help

I haven’t really done this, mainly because this is the first week I got stuck and my coach is away, heh.

Improving your process with reflection

I still feel a little silly doing this, but it’s becoming more useful.
